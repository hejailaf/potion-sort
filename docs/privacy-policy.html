Potion Sort — Privacy Policy

Last updated: July 6, 2026

Potion Sort has no accounts and asks for nothing personal. Your game progress (levels, coins, lives, settings) is stored only on your device. Here is everything the app does that involves data:

Anonymous analytics

We use Aptabase, a privacy-first analytics service, to understand how the game is played — events like "level won" or "booster used", with the level number and app version. These events are anonymous: no names, emails, precise locations, advertising identifiers, or persistent device IDs are collected, and nothing links sessions to you as a person.

Rewarded ads (optional)

Some buttons let you choose to watch a video ad for an in-game reward (an extra life or booster). Ads are served by Google AdMob. The first time you use one, iOS shows Apple's App Tracking Transparency prompt:

  • If you allow tracking, AdMob may use your device's advertising identifier (IDFA) to show more relevant ads, under Google's privacy policy.
  • If you decline, ads still work but are not personalized.

Ads never play automatically — only when you tap a "Watch ad" button.

In-app purchases

Coin packs are sold through Apple's App Store. Payment is handled entirely by Apple; we never see your payment details. We use RevenueCat to validate purchases; it processes your purchase receipt and an anonymous app-generated ID, not your identity.

Game Center

If you are signed in to Apple's Game Center, your highest level and achievements are submitted to your Game Center profile under Apple's privacy terms. If you're not signed in, nothing is sent.

Notifications

The app can remind you locally when your lives are refilled or a daily challenge awaits. These notifications are scheduled entirely on your device — no push servers, no remote messages — and only if you grant notification permission. You can revoke it any time in Settings.

What we never collect

  • No accounts, names, emails, or contacts
  • No precise location
  • No photos, microphone, or camera access
  • No selling of data to anyone

If you install a beta version through Apple TestFlight, Apple may share crash logs and feedback you choose to submit with the developer, under Apple's own privacy terms.
